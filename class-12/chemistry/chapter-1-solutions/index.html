Quick Revision Notes

What is a solution?

A solution is a homogeneous mixture of two or more substances that do not react chemically with one another.

Why concentration terms matter

Different concentration units are useful in different situations. Molarity is common in laboratories, while molality is preferred when temperature changes are important.

Henry's law

At a fixed temperature, the solubility of a gas in a liquid is directly proportional to the pressure of that gas above the liquid.

Colligative properties

These depend on the number of solute particles present in a solution rather than on the chemical identity of the solute.

NCERT Exercise Solutions

These exercise questions are presented in a cleaner format to support regular study, revision, and quick concept review for Chapter 1.

Exercise 2.1

Question: Define a solution. How many types of solutions are formed? Explain briefly with examples.

Answer: A solution is a homogeneous mixture of two or more non-reacting substances. Based on the physical states of solute and solvent, there are nine common types of solutions, including gas in gas, liquid in liquid, solid in solid, gas in liquid, and other combinations such as hydrogen in palladium or sugar in water.

Exercise 2.2

Question: If one substance has very small particles and the other has much larger particles, what kind of solid solution is likely to form?

Answer: Such a mixture is likely to form an interstitial solid solution, where the smaller particles occupy the spaces between larger particles.

Exercise 2.3

Question: Define mole fraction, molality, molarity, and mass percentage.

Answer: Mole fraction is the ratio of moles of one component to total moles. Molality is the number of moles of solute per kilogram of solvent. Molarity is the number of moles of solute per litre of solution. Mass percentage is the mass of solute present in 100 g of solution.

Exercise 2.4

Question: Find the molarity of laboratory nitric acid that is 68% by mass and has density 1.504 g mL^-1.

Answer: Consider 100 g of solution, convert the mass of HNO3 into moles, and use density to calculate solution volume. Molarity is then moles divided by volume in litres.

Exercise 2.5

Question: For a 10% by mass glucose solution in water, calculate molality, mole fraction, and molarity when density is 1.2 g mL^-1.

Answer: Start with 100 g of solution, so glucose = 10 g and water = 90 g. Convert each to moles for mole fraction and use 0.09 kg of solvent for molality. Use density to get volume and then calculate molarity.

Exercise 2.6

Question: How many mL of 0.1 M HCl are needed to react completely with 1 g of a mixture of Na2CO3 and NaHCO3 present in equimolar amounts?

Answer: Find the equimolar amounts first, calculate total moles of HCl required from the balanced reactions, and then use Volume = moles / molarity.

Exercise 2.7

Question: Find the percentage composition by mass after mixing 300 g of a 25% solution with 400 g of a 40% solution.

Answer: Calculate the solute mass in each portion, add them, divide by total mass of the mixture, and convert the result into percentage.

Exercise 2.8

Question: An antifreeze solution contains 222.6 g of ethylene glycol in 200 g of water. Find molality and molarity if density is 1.072 g mL^-1.

Answer: Convert ethylene glycol to moles. Use 0.2 kg of water for molality. For molarity, calculate the total solution volume from its mass and density.

Exercise 2.9

Question: Drinking water contains chloroform at 15 ppm by mass. Express this in percent by mass and determine the molality.

Answer: Fifteen ppm means 15 parts in one million parts by mass. Convert this to a percentage and then use the mass of chloroform in a notional 1 kg or 1,000,000 g sample to calculate molality.

Exercise 2.10

Question: What role do molecular interactions play in a solution of alcohol in water?

Answer: Alcohol and water both show hydrogen bonding. However, the alcohol-water interactions are weaker than the strong interactions in the pure liquids, so the solution tends to show positive deviation from Raoult's law.

Exercise 2.11

Question: Why do gases become less soluble in liquids when temperature increases?

Answer: Gas dissolution is usually exothermic. When temperature rises, the equilibrium shifts to oppose the added heat, so less gas remains dissolved.

Exercise 2.12

Question: State Henry's law and list important applications.

Answer: Henry's law states that at constant temperature, the solubility of a gas in a liquid is directly proportional to the pressure of that gas above the liquid. Applications include bottling soft drinks under pressure, breathing gas mixtures for deep-sea divers, and explaining oxygen transport in the body.

Exercise 2.13

Question: If a known mass of ethane produces a partial pressure of 1 bar, what will be the pressure for a larger dissolved mass?

Answer: Apply Henry's law directly. Since pressure is proportional to mole fraction, increase the pressure in the same ratio as the amount of dissolved ethane when the solvent amount remains constant.

Exercise 2.14

Question: Explain positive and negative deviations from Raoult's law and relate them to the sign of enthalpy of solution.

Answer: Positive deviation occurs when solute-solvent attractions are weaker than those in the pure components, so vapour pressure increases and the enthalpy change of solution is positive. Negative deviation occurs when solute-solvent attractions are stronger, so vapour pressure decreases and the enthalpy change is negative.

Exercise 2.15

Question: An aqueous solution containing 2% non-volatile solute exerts a pressure of 1.004 bar at the boiling point of the solvent. Find the molecular mass of the solute.

Answer: Use relative lowering of vapour pressure from Raoult's law to determine moles of solute, then calculate molecular mass from the given mass percentage.

Exercise 2.16

Question: Heptane and octane form an ideal solution. Find the vapour pressure for a mixture of 26.0 g heptane and 35.0 g octane at 373 K.

Answer: Convert masses to moles, calculate mole fractions in the liquid phase, and then use Raoult's law to find the total vapour pressure.

Exercise 2.17

Question: Calculate the vapour pressure of a 1 molal solution of a non-volatile solute in water at 300 K when pure water has vapour pressure 12.3 kPa.

Answer: Determine the mole fraction of water in a 1 molal solution and apply Raoult's law to obtain the new vapour pressure.

Exercise 2.18

Question: Calculate the mass of a non-volatile solute of molar mass 40 g mol^-1 that must be dissolved in 114 g of octane to reduce the vapour pressure to 80% of the original value.

Answer: A reduction to 80% means the relative lowering is 20%. Use mole fraction relationships from Raoult's law to find required moles of solute and then convert to mass.

Exercise 2.19

Question: A non-volatile solute is dissolved in water and vapour pressure changes after extra water is added. Find the molar mass of the solute and vapour pressure of pure water.

Answer: Form two Raoult's law equations using the two solution states. Solve them simultaneously to obtain the unknown molar mass and the vapour pressure of pure water.

Exercise 2.20

Question: A 5% cane sugar solution freezes at 271 K. Estimate the freezing point of a 5% glucose solution.

Answer: Compare the molalities of the two solutions at the same mass percentage. Since glucose and cane sugar have different molar masses, the freezing point depression changes accordingly.

Exercise 2.21

Question: Two compounds AB2 and AB4 produce different freezing point depressions in benzene. Find the atomic masses of A and B.

Answer: Use the freezing point depression data to calculate molar masses of AB2 and AB4, then solve the resulting simultaneous equations for A and B.

Exercise 2.22

Question: At 300 K, a glucose solution with 36 g per litre has osmotic pressure 4.98 bar. What concentration will produce 1.52 bar?

Answer: At constant temperature, osmotic pressure is directly proportional to concentration. Use the ratio of pressures to find the new concentration.

Exercise 2.23

Question: Suggest the most important intermolecular attractive force in each given pair.

Answer: Non-polar pairs such as n-hexane and n-octane, or iodine and carbon tetrachloride, show London dispersion forces. Ionic compounds in water show ion-dipole interactions. Polar compounds like methanol and acetone mainly show dipole-dipole interactions.

Exercise 2.24

Question: Arrange cyclohexane, KCl, CH3OH, and CH3CN in increasing order of solubility in n-octane.

Answer: Since n-octane is non-polar, the increasing order is: KCl < CH3OH < CH3CN < cyclohexane.

Exercise 2.25

Question: Identify which of the listed compounds are insoluble, partially soluble, or highly soluble in water.

Answer: Toluene and chloroform are insoluble, phenol and pentanol are partially soluble, and formic acid plus ethylene glycol are highly soluble because they form stronger interactions with water.

Exercise 2.26

Question: If lake water contains 92 g of Na+ ions per kg of water, calculate the molality of Na+ ions.

Answer: Convert 92 g of sodium ions into moles using the atomic mass of sodium, then divide by 1 kg of water.

Exercise 2.27

Question: If the solubility product of CuS is 6 x 10^-16, calculate its maximum molarity in water.

Answer: For CuS, let solubility be s. Then Ksp = s^2. Solve for s to obtain the molarity.

Exercise 2.28

Question: Calculate the mass percentage of aspirin in acetonitrile when 6.5 g is dissolved in 450 g of solvent.

Answer: Total mass of solution = 456.5 g. Therefore, mass percentage of aspirin = (6.5 / 456.5) x 100 = 1.424%.

Exercise 2.29

Question: Nalorphene is given as a 1.5 mg dose. Calculate the mass of a 1.5 x 10^-3 m aqueous solution needed.

Answer: Convert the dose into moles, then use the given molality to determine the mass of solvent and total mass of the required solution.

Exercise 2.30

Question: Calculate the amount of benzoic acid needed to prepare 250 mL of 0.15 M solution in methanol.

Answer: Required moles = 0.15 x 0.25 = 0.0375 mol. Multiply by molar mass of benzoic acid to get the required mass.

Exercise 2.31

Question: Explain why the depression in freezing point increases in the order acetic acid < trichloroacetic acid < trifluoroacetic acid.

Answer: The electron-withdrawing effect increases from acetic acid to trifluoroacetic acid, so ionization in water increases in the same order. More particles are formed, which leads to greater freezing point depression.

Exercise 2.32

Question: Calculate the depression in freezing point of water when 10 g of CH3CH2CHClCOOH is added to 250 g of water.

Answer: The source solution gives the final depression as approximately 0.65 K after accounting for partial dissociation and the Van't Hoff factor.

Exercise 2.33

Question: Find the Van't Hoff factor and dissociation constant of fluoroacetic acid from freezing point depression data.

Answer: First calculate the ideal molality, then use the observed depression to find the Van't Hoff factor. The dissociation constant can then be obtained from the degree of dissociation.

Exercise 2.34

Question: Vapour pressure of water at 293 K is 17.535 mm Hg. Find the vapour pressure after dissolving 25 g of glucose in 450 g of water.

Answer: Calculate the mole fraction of water and multiply it by the vapour pressure of pure water according to Raoult's law.

Exercise 2.35

Question: Henry's law constant for methane in benzene at 298 K is 4.27 x 10^5 mm Hg. Calculate its solubility under 760 mm Hg.

Answer: Using p = KHx, the mole fraction of methane comes out to about 1.78 x 10^-3.

Exercise 2.36

Question: Determine the vapour pressure of pure liquid A and its pressure in solution when 100 g of A is dissolved in 1000 g of B.

Answer: The source calculation gives the vapour pressure of pure liquid A as approximately 280.7 torr, while its partial pressure in the solution is 32 torr.

Exercise 2.37

Question: Plot vapour pressure data for acetone and chloroform and identify the deviation from ideal behaviour.

Answer: The experimental curve bends downward relative to the ideal line, showing negative deviation from Raoult's law.

Exercise 2.38

Question: Benzene and toluene form an ideal solution. Find the mole fraction of benzene in the vapour phase when 80 g benzene is mixed with 100 g toluene.

Answer: The source solution calculates the vapour-phase mole fraction of benzene as approximately 0.60.

Exercise 2.39

Question: Air is in equilibrium with water at 10 atm. Find the composition of oxygen and nitrogen dissolved in water.

Answer: The source calculation gives the mole fraction of oxygen in water as 4.61 x 10^-5 and nitrogen as 9.22 x 10^-5.

Exercise 2.40

Question: Determine the amount of CaCl2 dissolved in 2.5 L of water so that osmotic pressure is 0.75 atm at 27 C. Given i = 2.47.

Answer: The required amount of calcium chloride is approximately 3.42 g.

Exercise 2.41

Question: Determine the osmotic pressure of a solution prepared by dissolving 25 mg of K2SO4 in 2 L of water at 25 C, assuming complete dissociation.

Answer: First determine the Van't Hoff factor for complete dissociation, then use pi = iCRT with the very dilute concentration of the salt solution.
